# Hypothesis: Voynich as a Pre-Indo-European Remnant Language

## The Theory

**Voynich may be written in a now-extinct language that:**
1. Descended from the same ancestor as Basque/Aquitanian
2. Was a "sister" or "cousin" language to Basque
3. Died out with the spread of Latin and Indo-European languages
4. Survived long enough to be recorded in the 15th century

| Feature | Basque | Voynich | Interpretation |

| Matches | 17 words | - | Shared ancestry |
| Unique to Basque | 6 words | 6 Voynich-unique | Diverged vocabulary |
| Grammar | Basque system | Different system | Diverged grammar |

**A sister language would share SOME vocabulary but not all.**

### 2. Shared Roots with Different Derivatives

| Root | Basque | Voynich | Status |
|------|--------|---------|--------|
| ar | ar (male) | ar (346) | SHARED |
| or | or (dog) | or (350) | SHARED |

But Voynich also has extensive vocabulary NOT in Basque:
- ch- forms (1696 tokens) - not Basque
- sh- forms (1072 tokens) - not Basque
- qo- prefix (4991 tokens) - not Basque

### 3. Agglutinative Structure (Shared Feature)

Both Basque and Voynich are **agglutinative**:

| Feature | Basque | Voynich |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Suffixes | -ak, -ari, -ekin | -aiin, -ain, -dy, -ol |
| Prefixes | Limited | qo-, o-, y- |
| Root+affix | Yes | Yes |

**Same structural type, different specific morphemes.**

This is exactly what we'd expect from **related but divergent** languages.

### 4. Missing Core Basque Vocabulary

If Voynich WERE Basque, we'd expect these words:

| Basque | Meaning | Voynich |

## The Vasconic Substrate Theory

### What Linguists Have Proposed

Some linguists (Theo Vennemann, et al.) proposed a **Vasconic substrate** across Western Europe:

- Place names with Basque-like elements
- River names (hydronyms) with non-IE roots
- Mountain names with -aran, -ara patterns

### Geographic Distribution

Pre-IE languages may have existed across:
- Iberian Peninsula (Basque territory today)
- Southern France (Aquitaine, Gascony)
- Northern Italy (Liguria?)
- Parts of the Alps
- Possibly further

## Evidence AGAINST the Theory

### 1. Why Would It Survive to 1400s?

The Roman conquest was complete by ~19 BCE. For a language to survive until 1404-1438 (Voynich dating):

- ~1400 years of survival needed
- Under Latin/Romance pressure
- Would require extreme isolation

**Counter-argument**: Basque DID survive this long, proving it's possible.

### 2. No Historical Attestation

We have NO records of any language matching Voynich in medieval Europe.

**Counter-argument**:
- Many languages died unrecorded
- Voynich itself might BE the only record
- Basque was barely documented before 16th century

### 3. Turkish/Hungarian Vocabulary

Voynich also shows vocabulary from:
- Turkish (17+ matches)
- Hungarian (12 matches)
- Hebrew (8 matches)

A pure Vasconic language shouldn't have these.

**Counter-argument**:
- Trade contact could introduce loanwords
- Medieval Pyrenees were on trade routes
- Basque itself has Romance/Latin loanwords

## A Refined Hypothesis

### Scenario: Pyrenean Trade Pidgin

What if Voynich represents a **trade language** used in the Pyrenees/Mediterranean that:

1. Had a **Vasconic/Aquitanian core grammar**
2. Borrowed vocabulary from **trade partners**:
   - Turkish/Mongol (Silk Road merchants)
   - Hungarian (Central European trade)
   - Hebrew (Sephardic merchants)
3. Preserved **pre-IE roots** from Basque family
4. Developed **unique grammatical features**

## Testing the Hypothesis

### What Would Confirm It?

1. **More Basque-specific vocabulary** in undeciphered portions
2. **Grammatical patterns** matching Basque more closely
3. **Place names** or **personal names** with Basque structure
4. **Botanical terms** matching Basque plant names

### What Would Refute It?

1. **Complete decipherment** revealing non-Vasconic structure
2. **Discovery** of the language in medieval records
3. **Proof** that Voynich is a cipher of known language
